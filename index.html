Abstract

Spectroscopy is the gold standard for classifying stars by temperature, but it is slow and expensive: each star must be observed individually and its light dispersed into a full spectrum. Broadband photometry—simply measuring brightness through a handful of colored filters—is orders of magnitude cheaper and already exists for billions of objects. This project asks whether machine learning can recover a star's spectral class from photometry alone.

Using 93,628 spectroscopically-labelled stars from SDSS DR17, I engineer eleven color and magnitude features and train three classifiers (Random Forest, XGBoost, a multilayer perceptron) against a classical color-cut baseline. The headline result: ML lifts K-type recall from 46% to 91% and K-type F1 from 51% to 90%—a result with real operational value for exoplanet host-star surveys. At the same time, the persistent F/G confusion near 6,000 K is shown to be an irreducible physical limit of broadband photometry rather than a modelling failure, neatly separating what ML can fix from what only a spectrograph can.

01

Introduction & Motivation

Stellar classification is one of the oldest organizing tasks in astronomy. The Harvard spectral sequence—O, B, A, F, G, K, M—orders stars by surface temperature, from blistering blue O-type stars hotter than 30,000 K down to cool red M-dwarfs below 3,700 K. Our own Sun is a G-type star sitting comfortably in the middle.

Why spectroscopy is expensive

Historically, classifying a star meant taking its spectrum: dispersing its light into a rainbow and reading the absorption lines that fingerprint each temperature regime. This is precise—but costly. A spectrograph collects light from one object (or, with fibers, a few hundred) at a time, needs long exposures to gather enough photons for a faint star, and demands substantial instrument time. Spectroscopy simply does not scale to the billions of stars in modern surveys.

Why photometry is cheap—and tempting

Photometry is the opposite. You point a wide-field camera at a patch of sky, slide a filter in front of the detector that passes one slice of the spectrum, and record how bright each star is. SDSS does this in five bands—u g r i z, spanning 354 to 913 nm—imaging millions of objects per night. The difference between two bands, a color index such as g − r, tracks where a star's blackbody curve peaks, which is a direct proxy for temperature: hot stars are blue (small g−r), cool stars are red (large g−r).

The opportunity is clear. If a model could map five cheap brightness measurements to a spectral class reliably, it could be unleashed on the billions of stars in current and next-generation imaging surveys—LSST/Rubin, Euclid—where spectroscopy will never be available. Crucially, SDSS also took spectra for a large subset of its imaged stars, giving us ground-truth labels to train and test against.

Core thesis

ML on photometric colors yields genuine, operationally useful gains for some classes—dramatically so for K-type stars—while the F/G boundary near 6,000 K remains fundamentally unresolvable without spectroscopy. The interesting science is in separating the two.

Three questions

  • Performance. Can ML trained on SDSS color indices beat classical color-cut classifiers across the full OBAFGKM sequence?
  • Limits. Where does photometric classification fundamentally break down, and why?
  • Utility. For which spectral class does ML deliver the most operationally useful improvement over the baseline?
02

Methodology

2.1 · Data acquisition

Data were pulled from the SDSS SkyServer via an ADQL query joining the SpecObj and PhotoObj tables, selecting objects classed as STAR with a well-formed subClass. Quality cuts kept only reliable photometry: all five magnitudes between 14 and 23 (avoiding saturation at the bright end and the survey depth limit at the faint end), per-band uncertainties below 0.2 mag, and primary detections only (mode = 1) to drop duplicate observations. After parsing and cleaning, the working sample is 93,628 stars.

2.2 · Label parsing

Raw SDSS subClass strings cram spectral type and luminosity class together in inconsistent notation: G2V, K1III, B0.5Iae. Training on those raw strings would create hundreds of messy targets that confuse temperature with evolutionary stage. A custom parser strips this down to the temperature-relevant core—the leading letter plus its integer subtype—discarding luminosity suffixes and rejecting non-main-sequence objects (white dwarfs, subdwarfs, carbon stars). This yields two clean labels: spectral_subclass (e.g. G2, the ML target) and spectral_class (e.g. G, used for evaluation). The parsing produces 53 distinct subtypes.

2.3 · Feature engineering

Eleven features were built from the five magnitudes—six color indices plus the five raw magnitudes:

u−g · g−r · r−i · i−z · u−r · g−i

Color indices. Distance-independent: they depend only on the shape of the spectral energy distribution, which temperature sets. The workhorses of the model.

u · g · r · i · z

Raw magnitudes. Carry extra luminosity/distance information but introduce scatter. Included so the model can weigh them itself.

2.4 · Training strategy

  • Granular subclass training. Models train on subtypes (G2, K1) rather than coarse letters, preserving the continuous temperature gradient—the model learns that G0 sits closer to F9 than to G9. Predictions are coarsened to letters only after the fact for evaluation, never before.
  • Rare-class filtering. Subtypes with fewer than 20 examples are dropped before fitting; they are too sparse to learn and break stratified cross-validation.
  • Honest imbalance handling. Rather than fabricating stars with SMOTE—which would violate the true astrophysical class frequencies—I use class_weight='balanced' and cost-sensitive boosting. Rare O/B misclassifications are penalized more heavily without inventing data.
  • Split. 70 / 10 / 20 train / validation / test, stratified by subtype. Validation is reserved solely for threshold tuning; the test set is untouched until final evaluation.

2.5 · Models

  • Baseline — classical color cuts. Fixed threshold rules on g−r and u−g, mirroring pre-ML practice. No training, fully interpretable. The bar every ML model must clear.
  • Random Forest. 300 trees, balanced class weights, all 11 features. Handles mixed feature importance natively, needs no scaling, and exposes interpretable importances. 5-fold cross-validated.
  • XGBoost. Gradient-boosted trees (300 rounds, depth 6, lr 0.1). Labels integer-encoded post rare-class filtering—a necessary detail since XGBoost requires gap-free class indices.
  • MLP. A [128, 64, 32] fully-connected network with ReLU and early stopping, behind a StandardScaler since neural nets are scale-sensitive in a way trees are not.
  • Post-hoc threshold tuning. Per-class probability multipliers tuned on validation to curb systematic F over-prediction at G's expense; applied to the tree models, whose raw probabilities are less calibrated than the MLP's softmax.

A note on integrity: the spectroscopic effective temperature elodieTEff was deliberately excluded from training. Since spectral class is essentially a binning of temperature, including it would be direct data leakage—the model would be handed the answer.

Results

3.1 · Overall performance

All three ML models clear the baseline comfortably on weighted F1, precision, and recall across the full sequence. The gains concentrate in the cooler classes (K, M); O and B see little movement because their extreme blue colors already make them photometrically isolated and easy for even the naive baseline.

3.2 · The F/G confusion problem

The most stubborn failure mode—shared by every model including the baseline—is F-versus-G confusion. Across the ML models, roughly 28–34% of true G stars are misclassified as F, while F itself is recovered at ~89–91%. The asymmetry is consistent: F over-predicted, G under-predicted.

This is not a bug to be tuned away. The F/G boundary sits near 6,000 K, corresponding to g−r ≈ 0.45. SDSS photometric uncertainties of 0.02–0.05 mag in g and r translate to temperature uncertainties of several hundred Kelvin right at that boundary. Late-F stars (F8, F9) and early-G stars (G0, G1) are genuinely indistinguishable in broadband colors. Resolving them needs spectroscopy—the Ca II H&K lines or the Mg b triplet that straddle 6,000 K. The F/G wall is a physical limit of the data, not a deficiency of the model.

3.3 · K-type classification — the headline result

The most operationally significant finding is the leap in K-type classification. K stars matter: they are long-lived, stable, and the most common hosts targeted in exoplanet surveys—cool enough for interesting molecular features but without the violent variability of M dwarfs. Cleanly pre-selecting them from imaging alone is genuinely useful.

The recall jump is the striking part. The baseline misses more than half of all true K stars because its fixed g−r thresholds can't capture K stars sitting near the G/K or K/M boundaries. The ML models, working in the full 11-dimensional space, learn that combinations of r−i, i−z, and g−i isolate the K regime far more reliably than any single color. At ~90% precision and ~91% recall, when a model flags a star as K it is right roughly 9 times in 10, and it catches roughly 9 of every 10 real K stars—a candidate list that is both clean and complete enough to drive spectroscopic follow-up.

3.4 · Feature importance

Importances from the tree models line up with physics, which is a good sign the models learned something real rather than an artifact. g−r dominates (≈25–35% of total importance), exactly as expected for the primary temperature proxy. r−i and i−z follow, doing the heavy lifting for K/M separation where near-infrared molecular bands bite. u−g matters mainly for the hot O/B stars where the UV excess is diagnostic. Raw magnitudes contribute less than colors throughout—consistent with distance-dependent features adding noise.

04

Conclusions

What worked

ML on SDSS photometric colors delivers real, operationally useful gains over classical color cuts—but not uniformly. K-type classification is the clear success: balanced class weighting, a rich 11-dimensional feature space, and subclass-granular training combine to identify ~91% of K stars at ~90% precision. For survey astronomy that means photometry alone can pre-select K-star candidates reliably, cutting the spectroscopic burden for exoplanet host studies. The decision to train on granular subtypes and coarsen only afterward paid off in smoother, better-placed decision boundaries.

Honest limitations

The F/G confusion is the project's most honest result. Despite threshold tuning, oversampling experiments, and multiple architectures, every model misclassifies 28–34% of G stars as F—the expected behavior of any photometric classifier at the 6,000 K boundary. It should be read as confirmation of a known astrophysical constraint, not a shortfall. O and B stars stay hard for a different reason—they are genuinely rare (<1% combined), so even balanced weighting leaves modest recall. And the labels themselves come from ELODIE template matching, which carries its own boundary-region uncertainty, meaning a fraction of the "ground truth" is itself fuzzy.

Where this goes next

  • Gaia parallaxes. Adding absolute magnitudes injects a luminosity dimension that could break the F/G degeneracy by separating subgiants from main-sequence stars near 6,000 K—the single most promising next step.
  • A dedicated K-star catalogue tool. A binary K-vs-rest classifier optimized for recall could plausibly clear 95%, which would be publishable as a photometric K-star selection utility.
  • Per-star interpretability. SHAP values would decompose individual predictions, making the classifier auditable for downstream astrophysical use.
  • Generalization tests. Running the trained models on SDSS Stripe 82 (deeper, repeat photometry) would test whether photometric precision is the F/G bottleneck; applying them to simulated LSST/Rubin photometry would probe next-generation transfer.
Concluding statement

Broadband photometric ML is a reliable tool for K-type identification (F1 ≈ 90%, +39 pp over classical cuts) and a useful pre-classifier across OBAFGKM—while the F/G boundary near 6,000 K stays fundamentally unresolvable without spectroscopy. The results sit exactly where the physics says they should, and point to Gaia parallax integration as the most productive way forward.
